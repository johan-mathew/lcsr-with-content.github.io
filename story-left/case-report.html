LCSR Case Report

Faheema Shirin, a 3 rd semester B.A student of the Sree Narayanaguru College, Chelannur, Kozhikode was troubled by a rule change instituted by the college authorities which would have her and her fellow students compulsorily surrender their mobile phones between 6 pm and 10 pm. The rule would have her surrender a crucial learning aid that most of the students of her age take for granted. Faheema and a group of friends approached the authorities, yet was met with a cold shoulder, and when it became clear that she was not going to relent, the authorities turned increasingly hostile, threatening that any student who couldn’t abide by their rules could vacate their hostels. Even after meeting with her parents, who stood by Faheema’s demands, the authorities stubbornly refused to roll back their rule and in the end Faheema was forced to vacate her hostel room.

With the college authorities staying emboldened at what they viewed to be their victory, Faheema approached LCSR (Legal Collective for Student Rights) for legal recourse. The organization which has in previous years frequently represented student grievances identified several legal paradigms that the rule restricting mobile phone usage seemed to violate. Firstly, according to the plaintiff, the rule was exclusively exercised in the women’s hostel, which amounted to discrimination on the basis of gender, and thus was in violation of Clause 5 of UGC Student Entitlement Guidelines; the UGC (Promotion of Equity in Higher Education) Regulations, 2012; Conventions on Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination against Women, 1979 (“CEDAW”); Beijing Declaration; and the Universal Declaration of Human Rights. Furthermore, the restriction on the use of internet by students went against the Kerala Government’s policy of promoting digital learning from the school level, evident from the numerous policy declarations provided through official means and social media such as Facebook. Additionally, the rule deprived the student of her source of knowledge – the internet – which would be a detriment to her quality of education. Also, it could be argued that the right to access the internet forms a part of freedom of speech and expression guaranteed under Article 19(1)(a) of the Constitution of India, the restrictions of which covered by Article 19(2) cannot be extended to include the problematic rule devised and enforced by the college authorities. Moreover, the enforced surrender of her mobile phone is an invasion of a citizen’s fundamental right of privacy guaranteed under Article 21 of the Constitution of India. In addition, the modification of the rules governing the students primarily through parental conviction and assent could be argued to be an infringement of the personal autonomy of the adult student.

The plaintiff, represented by Adv. Legith T.Kottakkal, approached the High Court of Kerala, which presided over by Justice P.V Asha ruled in favour of the plaintiff. The Justice established in her judgement that “the students in a college hostel are adults who are capable of taking decisions …”, that “the college authorities as well as the parents cannot be permitted to shut their eyes on the innumerable advantages out of internet …”, and that “a student above the age of 18 years shall be given the freedom to choose the mode for her studies provided it does not cause any disturbance to others.” The Justice further states that the “enforcement of discipline shall not be by blocking the ways and means of the students to acquire knowledge” and ends with the caution to students that their “exercise [of right to privacy] does not invade the right to privacy of another student …”. The Justice in her concluding statements decreed that such restrictions were “unreasonable” and demanded that the plaintiff be re-admitted in the hostel without delay.

The implications of the judgement are obvious: it sets an important precedent for the use of internet within India, and has conferred on its citizens important legal ammunition against authoritarian control of the internet, be it by minor authorities or the State itself – another arrow has been gifted to the quiver of students’ rights.
